# Parkinson's Disease Overview

## What Parkinson's Disease Is

Parkinson's disease is a `progressive neurodegenerative disorder` most commonly associated with the loss of dopaminergic neurons in the `substantia nigra` and broader dysfunction across basal ganglia and related motor networks.

At a practical level, this means the disease affects:

- movement initiation and scaling
- movement speed and rhythm
- muscle stiffness
- balance and gait
- cognition, mood, sleep, autonomic function, and other non-motor systems

## Core Motor Features

The classic motor symptoms are:

- `bradykinesia`
  - slowness of movement and reduced movement amplitude
- `rigidity`
  - stiffness or increased resistance to passive movement
- `tremor`
  - often rest tremor, but not always
- `postural instability`
  - especially later in the disease

For this thesis, `bradykinesia` is especially important because it is closely related to:

- reduced amplitude
- impaired repetitive movement
- altered timing
- reduced manual dexterity

These are much closer to `finger tapping` and fine hand tasks than broad gait-only framing.

## Non-Motor Features

Parkinson's is not only a movement disorder.

Common non-motor domains include:

- sleep problems
- depression and anxiety
- fatigue
- constipation and autonomic symptoms
- pain
- cognitive changes
- apathy
- reduced sense of smell

This matters because disability in Parkinson's often comes from the interaction between motor and non-motor symptoms, not motor deficits alone.

## Who It Affects

- risk increases with age
- young-onset Parkinson's also exists
- men are affected slightly more often than women
- progression and symptom mix are highly heterogeneous

So there is no single `typical` Parkinson's patient in a precise engineering sense. That is one reason simplified thesis models must be framed carefully.

## How It Affects Daily Life

Upper-limb and hand problems can show up as:

- slower buttoning, writing, eating, and dressing
- reduced dexterity
- smaller and less regular repetitive finger movements
- fatigue during fine motor tasks
- greater difficulty under dual-task or attention-demanding conditions

This is one reason finger tapping remains clinically useful: it compresses several relevant movement deficits into a simple repetitive task.

## Treatment Buckets

Broadly, Parkinson management includes:

- `medication`
  - especially dopaminergic treatment such as levodopa
- `device-based therapy`
  - for example deep brain stimulation
- `rehabilitation`
  - physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy
- `exercise`
  - increasingly treated as a central management strategy
- `supportive and multidisciplinary care`

For this thesis, the most relevant comparison buckets are:

- rehabilitation
- exercise and task practice
- wearable or robotic assistance
- assessment and monitoring
- adaptive control systems

## What Matters Most For This Thesis

- Parkinson's hand impairment is real and clinically meaningful.
- `Finger tapping` is important mostly as an `assessment and task-definition` anchor.
- Parkinson-specific hand assistance papers are still relatively sparse.
- The broader PD literature is stronger in:
  - gait and cueing
  - monitoring and assessment
  - general rehabilitation logic
  - invasive closed-loop control

So the thesis will likely need to combine:

- Parkinson-specific `clinical framing`
- hand/finger rehab `mechanical precedent`
- simplified modeling and benchtop validation logic

## A Safe Summary Sentence

`Parkinson's disease is a heterogeneous neurodegenerative disorder with both motor and non-motor manifestations; for this thesis, the most relevant disease features are bradykinesia, reduced dexterity, impaired repetitive movement, and the rehabilitation logic built around repetitive task-oriented practice.`

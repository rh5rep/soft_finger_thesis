## Abstract / Summary

- This paper validates a smartphone-based timed finger-tapping application for quantitative assessment of Parkinson bradykinesia.
- Subjects alternately tapped two rectangles with the index finger at maximum speed for 10 seconds, which gives a very concrete example of a structured tapping test.
- The application produced movement measures that distinguished PD patients from controls and correlated with UPDRS-related metrics.
- This is not a wearable or robotic paper, but it is a useful baseline because it shows how little hardware is actually needed to create a direct tapping-assessment system.

## Model / Mechanism / Validation Details

- System type: smartphone-based tapping assessment app
- Actuation type: none
- Model type: timed touchscreen tapping test with kinematic summary variables
- Validation setup: 57 PD patients and 87 controls compared against standard methods including mechanical tappers
- Main metrics: mean number of correct taps, total distance moved, inter-tap distance, inter-tap dwell time, and correlation with UPDRS/bradykinesia scores

## What Matters For This Thesis

- This paper is useful as a lower-bound baseline for the assessment track.
- It helps explain that direct tapping-task technology is easy to build on the sensing side and much rarer on the exoskeleton side.
- It can strengthen the review's gap statement by showing that direct tapping quantification exists even when tapping-assist hardware does not.

## Limitations

- It is a phone app, not a wearable system and not an exoskeleton.
- It is about objective quantification, not assistance or stabilization.
- It should support the assessment side only and not consume too much presentation time.

## Decision Impact

- Keep as a compact baseline example rather than a headline paper.
- Use it to show the contrast between low-complexity assessment systems and high-complexity assistive hardware.

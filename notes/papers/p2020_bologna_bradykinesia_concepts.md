## Source-Verified Summary

- This is a review paper, not a device paper.
- It frames bradykinesia in Parkinson's disease as a multi-feature phenomenon rather than a single generic reduction in speed.
- The paper repeatedly links bradykinesia to slowness, reduced amplitude, sequence effect, and related initiation/hesitation problems, while arguing that these should be described more explicitly instead of being collapsed into one term.
- One of the most useful parts of the paper is that it explains why the current single-score clinical framing is too coarse: different abnormal features can appear in different combinations, vary by disease stage, and respond differently to treatment.
- The paper also contrasts the standard MDS-UPDRS style of global scoring with the MBRS style of separately rating speed, amplitude, and rhythm, which is directly relevant for how you describe finger-tapping outcomes.
- For the thesis, this matters because Parkinson finger tapping should not be reduced to one crude "speed" variable if the direct assessment literature already separates multiple deficit families.

## Conceptual Takeaways

- Terminology:
  - the review argues that `bradykinesia` is often used too broadly in both clinic and literature
  - historically it became an umbrella term that absorbed hypokinesia and akinesia
  - the authors argue that the sub-features should be described separately when possible
- Clinical rating logic:
  - MDS-UPDRS rates speed, amplitude, hesitations/halts, and decrement together across repeated movements
  - MBRS instead separates speed, amplitude, and rhythm, which is conceptually closer to the more granular assessment logic used in later task-analysis papers
- Lack of unity:
  - slowness, reduced amplitude, and sequence effect do not necessarily share one mechanism
  - they do not always co-occur in the same patient or body segment
  - feature profiles may vary with disease stage and with atypical parkinsonian syndromes
- Treatment implications:
  - levodopa improves slowed and reduced-amplitude movement, but does not reliably normalize sequence effect
  - the paper also discusses DBS as beneficial for some bradykinesia features while reinforcing that the mechanism is not one-dimensional
- Pathophysiology:
  - the paper rejects an overly narrow single-node explanation
  - it interprets bradykinesia as a network-level dysfunction involving basal ganglia, sensorimotor cortical areas, cerebellum, and altered sensorimotor processing
